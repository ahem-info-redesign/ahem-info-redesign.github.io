AHEM’S ASSABET RIVER HOMESCHOOL COOP

Posted March 14, 2019

If you are:

  • a homeschooling parent 18 years old or older,
  • a Friend of AHEM,
  • homeschool at least one child born in 2013 or earlier,
  • and live in one of the following towns:
    • Acton
    • Bedford
    • Boxborough
    • Carlisle
    • Concord
    • Lincoln
    • Littleton
    • Maynard
    • Stow
    • Sudbury
    • Westford

you are invited to join a coop designed to help independent homeschoolers get to know each other, share interests and skills, and plan social events, group activities, and field trips together!

The spring session will run from April 4 – June 6, 2019.

The cost is $50/family for the 10 week session.

To join:

  • you must agree to the Coop guidelines, which will be sent to you after you indicate interest and
  • you must commit to attending weekly park days, and to organizing an event or activity for the group, and commit to attending at least one such event.

If you would like to receive the Handbook and info on how to register, email info@ahem.info. Include your full street address.

Space in the coop is limited. No refunds.

HOMESCHOOL CONNECTIONS

Posted March 7, 2019

Thursday, March 28 6:45 to 8:45 pm in Watertown, MA

If you are looking to forge new relationships with fellow homeschoolers or are curious about homeschooling and want to learn more, you are invited to attend Homeschool Connections. The free program will be held in Watertown, MA on Thursday, March 28, 6:45 - 8:45 PM. Precise location will be sent when you register.

This meeting will provide a place for new and veteran homeschoolers and those considering homeschooling to ask questions, share experiences, and offer and receive support. Discussion topics will center around participants’ needs and curiosities.

The group will be facilitated by Stephanie Griego. Stephanie is on the board of Advocates for Home Education in Massachusetts, Inc., a non-profit 501(c)(3) charitable educational organization that gathers and disseminates information about homeschooling in Massachusetts through education, advocacy, and events.

Cost is $15/person to reserve your spot; the full price will be refunded to you after the meeting, making your participation FREE. If you don't show up, your money will be donated to support the non-profit mission of AHEM. No refunds except those made to attendees after the workshop on March 28th.

Register here. Space is limited and spots are allotted on a first come, first served basis.

Participants will be added to the Friends of AHEM list unless they specifically opt out. Friends of AHEM keep abreast of legislative and other issues that affect Massachusetts homeschoolers via a low volume announcement-only email list. Friends of AHEM get special notice of AHEM events. This is a free service. AHEM respects your privacy and will not share your name or contact information with any other organization.

Please respect AHEM’s mission to empower individuals through education by not soliciting for your business at this event.

Bespoke Homeschooling Without Breaking the Bank

Posted February 21, 2019

You’ve made the leap, cut the cord, took the plunge–however you put it--you’ve pulled your kid out of school, or made the decision not to send them at all. Often this decision means the family budget also takes a hit, as one or both parents adjust their schedules and/or work hours to spend more time with the children. Unschooling as a Feminist Act

Posted January 3, 2019

When I was a young mother, I wore a t-shirt with the words: “The hand that rocks the cradle rocks the boat.” The phrase put a spin on a 19th century poem entitled “The Hand That Rocks the Cradle Rules the World” by American poet William Ross Wallace. I understood at the time that becoming a mother was increasing my desire to create change in the world, although I didn’t know where that would lead me. I had already realized that, as the feminist movement espoused, the personal is political. I had already challenged a few assumptions about how life was supposed to work—including rejecting both the style in which I’d been parenting and the institution of school as an effective vehicle for education.
